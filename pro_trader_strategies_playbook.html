Golden rules of intraday
!Never trade 9:15–9:30 AM — this is the panic zone. Let the dust settle. Your entry comes after clarity, not chaos.

Strategy 2 — Breakout from consolidation
Range breakout + volume surge Medium risk, high reward
Win rate ~50% R:R 1:4 Hold 1–4 weeks
Stock consolidates in a tight range for 15–30+ days. The longer the base, the bigger the move. Volume contracts during consolidation (base building). On breakout day, volume should be 2–3× average — this confirms institutions are loading. Enter on breakout candle close. The best breakouts have very tight ranges and explosive volume.

Pro tip — buy the retest, not the breakout
After a breakout, price often comes back to test the breakout level (old resistance becomes new support). This retest entry has a tighter stop and better R:R than chasing the initial move. More patient, more profitable.

What makes me pull the trigger — buy signals
The 3-confirmation ruleCore rule
Never enter on 1 signal. Wait for 3 independent confirmations from 3 different categories (trend + momentum + volume). When all 3 align, the trade has high probability. When only 2 align, it is a watch. When only 1 aligns, do not trade.
Bullish entry signals — all need 3+ to confirm
Price closes above SMA 21 after a pullback — trend is resuming. The dip buyers are winning.
RSI crosses above 50 from below — momentum flipping bullish. Strongest when RSI was oversold first.
MACD histogram turns positive — buyers overtaking sellers in momentum. Very reliable signal.
Volume 1.5–2× average on a green candle — institutions are buying. Low volume moves are traps.
Bullish engulfing on daily chart at support — buyers overwhelm sellers in one candle. Very high probability reversal.
Stock breaking 52-week high — the clearest breakout signal. No overhead resistance. Only early sellers remain.
Golden cross (SMA50 crossing above SMA200) — rare but one of the highest conviction buy signals for positional trades.
FII buying stock in their portfolio — if you can see bulk/block deal data showing FII accumulation, ride with them.
Bearish signals — when I consider selling or shorting
Price breaks below SMA 50 with volume — uptrend is broken. Exit longs immediately.
RSI divergence — price makes new high but RSI doesn't — hidden weakness. Smart money selling into strength.
Death cross (SMA50 below SMA200) — macro trend is broken. Only short-term bounces should be traded long.
High volume red candle breaking support — distribution. Institutions are exiting. Retail is still buying — don't be them.
Shooting star or bearish engulfing at resistance — sellers overwhelming buyers at a key level. First sign of reversal.
OBV falling while price rises — major divergence. Volume not confirming the move. Exit or tighten stop.
Promoter pledge increasing two quarters in a row — insider stress. Exit before others find out.

The hardest skill — knowing when to exit
Core exit philosophy
Entries are easy. Exits make or break a trader. Most traders exit winners too early and hold losers too long — the exact opposite of what works. The goal: let winners run to target, cut losers fast at stop loss. No exceptions, no hope.

Trailing stop methods
ATR trailing stopBest for swing
Trail stop at (highest close − 2×ATR). As price moves up, stop moves up but never down. Gets you out when volatility suggests a real reversal, not just noise.
EMA trailing stopBest for trend
Trail stop just below the 21 EMA on your trading timeframe. As long as price stays above EMA21, hold. First close below EMA21 = exit. Simple and very effective.
Swing low trailingPosition trades
Move stop to just below each new higher swing low as price trends up. Manual but very powerful — keeps you in the trade through normal pullbacks while protecting profit.
Time-based exitIntraday only
For intraday: exit all positions by 3:15 PM regardless of profit/loss. For swing: if trade hasn't worked in 10 trading days, exit — the thesis may be wrong.

Risk control rules — the laws of survival
1Never average down a losing trade — adding to a loss is not a strategy, it is denial. The market is already telling you that you are wrong.
2Never remove a stop loss — once set, it stays. If the logic changes, exit the trade. Stop losses save accounts.
3No leverage until you are profitable for 6 months straight — leverage amplifies mistakes before it amplifies profits.
4Keep 30% cash always — markets crash and give incredible opportunities. You need dry powder to buy the dip.
5Hedge before results and budget — buy a put option or reduce position by 50% before binary events.
6Track every trade in a journal — entry, exit, setup type, emotion, result. Patterns in your losses will reveal your specific weakness.
7Review performance weekly, not daily — daily P&L watching causes emotional decisions. Weekly review reveals if your process is working.
The pro trader mindset — what separates 5% from 95%
The uncomfortable truth
95% of traders lose money. They all know the indicators. They all have charts. The difference is not knowledge — it is psychology, discipline, and process. A trader with average knowledge and excellent discipline will outperform a brilliant analyst with poor emotional control every single time.
Core mental laws
Process over outcome
A losing trade can be a good trade if you followed your rules. A winning trade can be a bad trade if you broke them. Judge yourself on process, not results. Over 100 trades, good process = profits.
Think in probabilities
No single trade matters. What matters is the edge over 50–100 trades. A 55% win rate with 1:2 R:R means you make money even being wrong 45% of the time. Accept losses as part of the business.
Patience is the edge
The best traders make 3–5 high-quality trades per week — not 30. Waiting for A-grade setups only and skipping B and C setups is what makes the difference. Most losses come from boredom trades.
Detach from money
Think in R-multiples, not rupees. A 1R win is a 1R win whether it is ₹1,000 or ₹1,00,000. The moment you think about what the money represents (rent, EMI, holiday), your decisions become emotional.
Every day is a clean slate
Yesterday's loss is irrelevant to today's trade. Trying to recover yesterday's loss today leads to revenge trading. Each trade stands alone on its own merit.
Keep a trading journal
Write your reason for entry, expected setup, emotion at entry, result, and what you would do differently. Review weekly. Your journal will show you patterns in your mistakes that no indicator can reveal.
Losses are tuition
Every losing trade paid for properly (with a stop loss, no overleveraging) is education. The only catastrophic loss is the one that breaks your account or your confidence so badly you quit.
Never trade to prove a point
If you predicted a stock would fall and it rose, do not short it out of stubbornness. The market does not know or care about your opinion. Adapt or lose money.
